## BARTENDING SCENARIOS (10)

### 1. The 'Surprise Me' Gin Guest
**Question:** The 'Surprise Me' guest says they like gin but are tired of a G&T. How do you ask clarifying questions, make a tailored recommendation, and then build it confidently?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd start by asking what flavour direction excites them — something citrus-forward and bright, or more herbal and complex? Once I know their preference, I'd recommend a Bee's Knees if they want something smooth and honeyed, or a Negroni if they're after bolder botanicals and spice. I'd build it with confidence, explain why it fits their brief, and deliver it with a garnish that enhances the spirit they came in for."

---

### 2. Three Orders at Once (Mojito, Guinness, Chardonnay)
**Question:** You have three orders at once: Mojito, Guinness, and house Chardonnay. Describe your exact workflow and mise-en-place so speed does not damage quality.

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd start the Guinness first since it needs time to settle properly. While that pours, I'd prep my mojito glassware, chill it quickly, and set up my lime and mint. Once the Guinness settles, I'd build and muddle the mojito, then pour the wine into a proper glass. This sequence protects the Guinness head, keeps the mojito fresh, and ensures the wine hasn't been sitting waiting. Each drink gets full attention in the right order."

---

### 3. Brandy Frappé
**Question:** A guest asks for a Brandy Frappé. Explain the recipe, correct glassware, and key technique to prepare this classic correctly.

**Perfect Answer:**
"A Brandy Frappé is a beautiful simple drink — brandy, crème de cacao, and a touch of lemon juice, shaken hard with crushed ice and served in a shallow coupe with more crushed ice. The crushed ice is essential because it creates that silky texture that sets it apart from a regular serve. I'd explain to the guest that it's refreshing, spirit-forward but balanced, and they can expect it chilled and smooth — not watered down, but luxuriously cold."

---

### 4. Margarita with Scotch Request
**Question:** A guest wants a Margarita made with Scotch. How do you guide them professionally toward a better fit while still making them feel heard?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd absolutely hear them out. I'd explain that Scotch's smoke and peat would overpower a margarita's citrus balance, but I can see they want something spirit-forward and bold. I'd suggest a Penicillin — Scotch, honey, lemon, and ginger — which gives them the peaty whisky they're after with beautiful balance. Or if they prefer citrus, a Whisky Sour with a premium Scotch would be superb. Both options respect their instinct while landing them in something that will genuinely taste better."

---

### 5. Six Cocktails 10 Minutes Before Close
**Question:** It is 10 minutes to closing and a group asks for six complicated cocktails. How do you balance hospitality, speed, and team communication?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd acknowledge them warmly: 'Thanks for coming in, we love late arrivals.' Then I'd be honest: 'Six cocktails will take about 15 minutes, but if you'd prefer, I can build two signature cocktails now that are stunning and serve them quickly to the whole group.' This sets real expectations, respects their time, and often leads them toward a smarter order. If they commit to six, I'd work with my team, batch prep, and communicate the timeline so we all move together and they're never waiting on a single drink."

### 6. Bourbon vs Rye Flight
**Question:** A guest wants to understand Bourbon vs Rye through a three-whiskey flight. What do you choose and how do you explain each pour?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd design this intentionally: a four-grain bourbon to show smooth sweetness, a high-rye bourbon to highlight where spice enters the bourbon category, and a straight rye to let them taste pure peppery intensity. As I pour, I'd explain: 'The first is sweet and approachable — that's the corn and age. The second brings rye spice into a bourbon structure, so you see the contrast. The third is rye itself — brighter, spicier, cleaner finish.' This teaches through direct comparison so they leave understanding the category, not just tasting three whiskies."

---

### 7. 'Strong but Smooth' Request
**Question:** A guest asks for a 'strong but smooth' cocktail. How do you recommend something that matches both requests without overwhelming them?

**Perfect Answer:**
"Strong and smooth are different textures, so I'd ask: are they after high alcohol presence or just bold flavour? Most want both. I'd recommend an Old Fashioned — spirit-forward, high alcohol, but the dilution and balance make it silky and warming rather than harsh. Or if they prefer something lighter-feeling, a Daiquiri with premium rum — high ABV but the citrus and dilution make it smooth and refreshing. I'd explain that the right dilution and balance make strength taste elegant, not aggressive."

---

### 8. Better Martini Explanation
**Question:** A guest asks why your Martini tastes better than usual. How do you explain vermouth handling, brand choice, and technique in a way that builds trust?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I appreciate that — thank you. It's three things working together: we use fresh, quality vermouth stored in the fridge so it hasn't oxidised, we chill our glassware and mixing glass deeply so every degree counts, and we stir it properly to chill and dilute it without bruising the spirit. That perfect dilution is what makes it silky. Some places skip these details, so you end up with warm vermouth and a thin, sharp drink. Ours tastes cleaner and rounder because the process protects the gin."

---

### 9. Intoxicated Guest Refusing Cutoff
**Question:** You believe a guest is intoxicated but they insist on another round of shots for the group. How do you decline service with tact and safety focus?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd step in calmly and speak quietly to the group: 'I can see the evening is going well, but I've got to look after everyone at the table. Let me bring over some water and some bar snacks — that's on us. We can absolutely keep the good vibes going, just letting everyone pace themselves so the night lasts.' I'd flag support from a manager if the group pushes back, but I'd stay calm and respectful because tone matters more than the refusal itself. Safety is non-negotiable, but delivery matters."

---

### 10. Sazerac Exact Spec
**Question:** A guest orders a Sazerac. Walk through the exact spec, including the rinse, spirit choice, and garnish protocol.

**Perfect Answer:**
"A Sazerac is a precision classic. I'd rinse a frozen rocks glass with absinthe, then chill it in ice. I'd stir rye whiskey — usually Sazerac brand rye — with a dash of Peychaud's bitters and a small piece of sugar until the ice melts and it's perfectly diluted and cold. I'd dump the rinse ice, strain the spirit into the absinthe-coated glass, express a lemon peel over it for oils, and serve it up. No garnish sits in the glass because it's about the spirit, bitters, and that absinthe aromatics. It's a simple drink done exactly right."

## SALES SCENARIOS (10)

### 1. Couple with Cocktails, No Food Menu
**Question:** A couple sits at the bar and orders two cocktails but has not opened the food menu. How do you introduce a snack or small plate in a way that feels helpful rather than pushy?

**Perfect Answer:**
"After they settle in with their drinks, I'd say: 'Those cocktails are beautiful together. What I'm loving tonight is our charcuterie board — it's a small grazing plate that's perfect if you want to settle in and chat for a bit. Want to try it?' If they're not ready, I'd drop it. If they're interested, I'd add: 'The cured meats pair perfectly with what you're sipping, and you can pick at it without commitment.' It feels like a helpful suggestion, not a push, because it's framed as a companion to the moment they're already having."

---

### 2. Table of Eight Undecided on Starters
**Question:** A table of eight is undecided and still reading menus. How do you recommend two appetizers for the table while positioning them as the best way to start?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd say: 'While you're deciding on mains, can I suggest starting with two things that pair beautifully together? Our seared scallops and the burrata — both are fantastic shared formats, and they'll arrive quickly so you've got something delicious while you finish deciding on the bigger plates.' This frames starters as something they'll want anyway, positions two specific recommendations, and gives them permission to take their time on mains without waiting hungry. I'd frame it as momentum, not upsell."

---

### 3. Sirloin Guest, Ribeye Premium Sell
**Question:** A guest orders the sirloin, but your ribeye is the stronger premium sell. How do you describe the ribeye so the upsell feels natural and justified?

**Perfect Answer:**
"Great choice — the sirloin is excellent. Can I just tell you about the ribeye? It's got richer marbling throughout, so you get more flavour and a buttery texture that sirloin doesn't always have. It's worth the difference if you're after that more indulgent experience. But the sirloin is fantastic — it's just leaner, a bit more straight-forward. My call? If today feels like a treat yourself moment, go ribeye. If you want a great steak that's leaner, sirloin is perfect." I'd keep control with them, not pressure, and let them decide."

---

### 4. "What's Good Tonight?" with Special and Pairing
**Question:** A guest asks, 'What's good tonight?' How do you answer by spotlighting one chef's special and pairing it with a wine or cocktail recommendation?

**Perfect Answer:**
"Tonight, the roasted duck with cherry gastrique is performing really well — it's tender, the sauce is beautiful, and the cherry brings a brightness that's seasonal right now. I'd pair it with the Côtes du Rhône if you're after wine, or if you want cocktail, our house negroni echoes the bittersweet profile and carries it really well. Both options amplify what the kitchen did tonight. I'd rather give you one standout recommendation with a real pairing than list everything and leave you guessing."

### 5. Too Full for Dessert, But Celebrating Birthday
**Question:** A table says they are too full for dessert, but you know they are celebrating a birthday. How do you pivot to a shared dessert without sounding salesy?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I can see the meal hit beautifully. Before I let you go, I just noticed it's a birthday — can we bring out a small shared moment? Something light and celebratory, just a few bites so you've got a memory of tonight. We're going to plate something small and special, no pressure, just our way of marking the occasion." This acknowledges fullness, respects their state, makes it about the celebration not the sale, and frames it as a gesture rather than an upsell. Most say yes because it's personal."

You're going to want that beside your cup. It's the classic pairing for exactly this moment." I'd frame it as a natural, expected pairing that will enhance the espresso, not something separate. A small dessert with coffee feels like continuity, not a hard sell."

### 7. House Wine Guest, Upsell to Premium
**Question:** A guest points to the house wine. How do you describe it briefly and then offer a taste of the next-tier premium glass without sounding pushy?

It's a bit more complex and structured. How about I pour both side by side so you can taste the difference? Then you decide which feels right for tonight." A taste eliminates risk and lets them experience the upgrade directly. Most people choose the premium once they've tasted it, because price suddenly makes sense."

### 8. Well Vodka Soda Upgrade

With soda, the spirit is the whole point, so the quality makes a real difference to how the drink sits. It's just two dollars more, and in a soda serve, premium vodka tastes genuinely better because you're not masking it behind a lot of mixers." I'd link the quality directly to the specific serve and explain the sensory difference, not just the price."

## MANAGEMENT SCENARIOS (20)

### 1. Junior Staff with Attitude, Stuck Running Food
**Question:** A junior staff member shows great attitude but limited experience and has been stuck running food for months. How do you create a stepped progression plan that keeps them engaged and gives them a path forward?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd have a conversation: 'I've noticed your attitude is genuinely great, and that matters more than experience. I want to build you toward something bigger. Here's my proposal: next month, we'll get you shadowing bar on two shifts to see how that role connects to what you're already doing. After that, we'll trial you on simple bar shifts. In three months, we'll check in on progress and talk about what comes next.' Clear steps, visible milestones, and ownership make people feel invested instead of stuck in one role forever."

---

### 2. Senior Technical Staff Hesitant About Leadership
**Question:** You have an experienced senior team member who is technically brilliant but hesitant to step into leadership. How do you back them and build their confidence?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd pull them aside: 'I see your skills and how the team naturally looks to you. Leadership isn't about knowing everything — it's about what you already do: staying calm, helping people get better, and showing up consistently. I want to give you a small first step: next service, you're on section lead for the back team. I'll debrief with you after to walk through what landed and what felt hard.' Small supported steps build confidence more than sudden titles. Then we gradually expand the scope."

---

### 3. Introducing Formal Progression Program
**Question:** Your company wants to implement structured progression plans like a formal development program. How do you introduce it to the team and get buy-in from skeptical staff?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd bring the team together: 'We're building a progression system so everyone can see how they grow here — from where you are now to the next role, what skills matter, what pay looks like. You're each going to work with me to set goals that actually matter to you, not generic ones. This isn't more admin; it's clarity around growth. We'd kick off with a one-on-one conversation about what you want to get better at and where you see yourself heading.' Tying it to personal upside and choice gets buy-in."

---

### 4. Overseas Staff Member, Visa Expiring in 8 Months
**Question:** A talented overseas staff member is invaluable, but their visa expires in 8 months. How do you start early conversations about sponsorship and a realistic pathway?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd schedule a sit-down early: 'Your visa timeline is on my radar. I want to have this conversation now so we can plan properly instead of scrambling later. Here's honestly what sponsorship would require from us — it's a commitment to make you permanent. Here's what we'd need to see from you in the next five months to make that case strong to ownership. Let's talk about whether this is something you both want to explore.' Early clarity and mutual investment usually lead to solutions."

---

### 5. Weekly 'I Learned' Reflection Practice
**Question:** You want to build a culture of reflection and continuous improvement. How do you implement a simple weekly 'I learned' practice without making it feel like homework?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd start in our Friday team huddle: 'Every Friday before we finish, I'm going to ask: What's one thing you learned this week from a guest, a problem, or working with each other? I'll share first so it feels safe.' Keep it to 30 seconds per person, stay genuine, and skip the formality. After a few weeks, people start volunteering. It normalizes learning as part of the job, not something separate. I model it so the team sees it as actually useful."

### 6. Strong Staff Member Resigns for Slightly More Pay, Cites Lack of Growth
**Question:** A strong staff member resigns for only slightly more pay, saying they did not feel valued or see growth opportunities. How do you use that feedback before others follow?

**Perfect Answer:**
"That departure is system feedback, not just a hire. I'd immediately review: Are we recognising wins? Are progression paths actually visible or just assumed? How often are we checking in on growth? Then I'd hold retention conversations with my key people in the next week: 'I want to be real about what growth, recognition, and opportunity look like here. What would make you feel valued?' That resignation might save three others from leaving because I'm now acting on the signal instead of ignoring it."

---

### 7. Five-Minute Pre-Shift Huddles
**Question:** You want to improve morale and alignment with 5-minute pre-shift check-ins. How do you make these huddles useful rather than another empty meeting?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd keep it structured: 'First, shout-out to someone who did something great last shift. Two, here's what's different today — we've got a big event, or the kitchen is running skeleton crew. Three, what's our service goal tonight?' Five minutes max. I'd open with one shout-out so people see it's genuine, mention one thing that matters operationally, name the goal, and go. Consistency matters — do it the same way every shift so people know what to expect and it becomes a ritual instead of interruption."

---

### 8. Excessive Hours During Busy Period, Protect Balance
**Question:** Your team has been working excessive hours during a busy period. How do you restructure rosters to protect work-life balance without losing service standards?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd acknowledge it first: 'I know the last month has been intense. That's not sustainable, and burnout doesn't serve anyone.' Then I'd review rosters to rotate who opens/closes, distribute the heaviest shifts, and enforce one day off per week. I'd talk to ownership: 'If we burn out our reliable people, we lose them. Sustainable rosters protect long-term service more than squeezing short-term.' Then I'd communicate the new roster as a decision, not a favor: 'This is how we're going to run ahead.' People respect that you're protecting them."

---

### 9. Cost-Free Peer Recognition System
**Question:** You want a simple, cost-free peer recognition system. How do you set it up so it feels authentic and builds appreciation rather than awkwardness?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd use a weekly moment in huddle: 'Who did something yesterday that made service better for someone else?' Then ask the nominator to be specific: 'What did they do exactly?' Not 'Great job,' but 'You jumped in when we got slammed and stayed calm, which let me focus on the difficult table.' I'd make a quick note and follow up with the person later: 'Your team noticed you stayed calm during the rush yesterday — that's the standard we're building.' Specific, genuine, quick, and noticed."

### 10. Pitch Culture Investment to Cost-Focused Owners
**Question:** Owners see team building as an expense rather than an investment. How do you make the case for allocating budget to retention-focused culture activities?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd use numbers: 'Replacing one mid-level person costs us about 8 thousand in recruiting, training, and lost standards while they ramp. We turn over four people a year, that's 32 thousand. A 1500-dollar quarterly team building thing, better schedule flexibility, and a small recognition budget we're proposing — total maybe 10 thousand — cuts that turnover in half. You've just saved money and kept standards. Culture spend is protecting revenue and consistency.' Owners speak ROI. Show them the business logic and they usually fund it."

### 11. Junior Staff Apologises for Senior Staff Attitude During Training

Training is tough, but frustration can't come across to customers. We stay professional and calm in front of guests, every time. Let's reset how you transfer knowledge so it's firm but not harsh.' Then I'd model working together positively on the floor so they see the standard."

### 13. Identify Interim Leader During Off-Site Crisis

### 14. Recently Promoted, Micromanaging Former Peers

### 15. During Crisis, Staff Froze or Abandoned Post
**Question:** During a busy crisis, one staff member froze or abandoned their post. After service, how do you distinguish between someone who tried but failed and someone who gave up?

**Perfect Answer:**
"I'd ask them: 'Walk me through what you saw happening. What did you think, and what did you try to do?' Listen for effort and logic. If they say 'I panicked and froze,' that's different from 'I didn't think it was my problem so I went on break.' Panic under pressure is coachable — we build their resilience. Disengagement is a character choice — that's a different conversation. Coaching focuses on capability, but accountability addresses choice. The distinction changes what I do next."
