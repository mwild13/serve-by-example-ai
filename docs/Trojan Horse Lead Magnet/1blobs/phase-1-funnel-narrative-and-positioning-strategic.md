## Pain narrative — Profile 1: Compliance-Driven Group Ops Manager

**Core fear:** An incident from three months ago resurfaces in a licensing review and no one can find the log. Or the log exists — on a bit of paper, half filled out, signed by a staff member who left six weeks ago.

**Daily friction moments:**

- Pulling compliance evidence for a multi-venue audit and finding every site uses a different format — or no format at all
- Chasing venue managers for their incident registers the week before a licensing inspection
- A new staff member serves a minor or an intoxicated patron and there is no documented evidence of their RSA training
- Trying to confirm whether each venue's logs meet the requirements of their state's Liquor Act when every site does it differently

**Native language:**

- "Our incident register isn't something I want the licensing officer finding"
- "If something goes wrong at one venue, I need a clean record in under two minutes"
- "Three venues, three different filing systems — and none of them talk to each other"
- "We had an incident, nothing came of it, but if they'd asked for our training records I'd have been screwed"
- "I need to hand this stuff to a barrister if it comes to that"

**The manual paper moment the toolkit surfaces:** The multi-site audit checklist forces the ops manager to score each venue against each state requirement. The scores make the exposure visible across all sites simultaneously. Inconsistency between venues is the finding. The fix (SBE) becomes structurally obvious.

## Pain narrative — Profile 2: Overworked Independent Owner-Operator

**Core fear:** Something goes wrong — an underage service, an intoxication incident — and they can't prove they trained their staff. Or they've lost two good people in a month because there was never time to train them properly and they felt unsupported.

**Daily friction moments:**

- Tuesday arvo: two casuals starting at 5pm, function at 7pm. RSA explained verbally in a 10-minute walk-through. No record kept. No sign-off. Hope for the best.
- Monday morning: writing the same house rules document for the fifth time this year because they can't find last month's version
- Trying to explain cocktail specs to a new bartender while simultaneously running the bar during a Friday service
- A staff member they trained up for three months gives two weeks notice. Everything they knew leaves with them.

**Native language:**

- "I'm the manager, the trainer, the barback, and the bloke fixing the keg lines"
- "I showed them the RSA stuff when they started — but that was in my head, nothing written down"
- "By the time I've explained everything, they're on shift and I'm already in the weeds"
- "I've rewritten that same induction document four times this year — every time I can't find the last one"
- "If Liquor & Gaming walks in tonight and asks about my training records, I've got nothing"

**The manual paper moment the toolkit surfaces:** The onboarding SOP with a time-cost estimator. Each section has an "estimated time per hire" column. The owner fills it in. A formula accumulates the total. Then it multiplies by their annual turnover number. By the end of the template, they have quantified exactly how many hours per year they personally spend on manual onboarding. The number is always embarrassing. SBE appears as the direct comparison.

## Pain narrative — Profile 3: Retention-Focused Venue Manager

**Core fear:** Losing good people because the venue can't get them up to speed fast enough. Staff feel unsupported and leave before they've found their footing. The manager watches it happen and has no system to stop it.

**Daily friction moments:**

- Week 3: a staff member who was showing real promise hands in their resignation. Feedback: "I just didn't feel supported."
- The Monday debrief that never happens — no system for it, just ad hoc feedback delivered on the pass during a Friday night service
- Senior staff burning out carrying new hires because there's no structured onboarding and someone has to fill the gap
- Watching a new bartender visibly struggle on a Friday night because they were put on service before they understood the specs or the floor

**Native language:**

- "We keep losing people in the first four weeks"
- "The senior staff are over it — they're carrying the new ones and it's wearing them down"
- "I can see they're struggling but I don't have time to shadow them every shift"
- "They get chucked on the floor and basically learn by getting yelled at"
- "If we could get people up to speed in two weeks instead of six, I'd keep more of them"
- "The good ones stay when they feel like the venue actually gives a shit"

**The manual paper moment the toolkit surfaces:** The FOH/BOH SOP template reveals — through the act of completion — how much is currently undocumented. Every section the manager cannot fill in ("we do this verbally"), every SOP that exists only in the head of one senior staff member. The incompleteness of the document is the pain. SBE appears as the system that holds this knowledge structurally.

## Pitch pivot structural pattern

**Principle:** Never sell inside the tool. The pivot works by making the cost of the manual process visible, then placing SBE in the user's peripheral vision as the resolution — without requesting anything.

### Position 1 — The friction counter

Embedded at the top of each major section as a simple formula table.

*RSA log version:* "Incident entries this quarter: [ ] | Average time per entry (mins): [ ] | Total quarterly admin time: [formula]"

*SOP version:* "Time to deliver this section verbally per new hire (mins): [ ] | New hires per year: [ ] | Annual hours on this section alone: [formula]"

The user supplies the numbers. The formula delivers the verdict. No commentary is added. The total is the pivot.

### Position 2 — The gap callout

A Notion callout block placed at each checklist item that is difficult to evidence manually. No SBE branding. Format:

> Most venues manage this with a spreadsheet or a paper log. This is one of the most common points of failure during a licensing inspection.

This functions as a functional annotation, not a sales message. It names the risk. It does not offer the solution yet.

### Position 3 — The resolution block

Appears once, at the bottom of each major asset section — after the friction counter and gap callouts have done their work.

Heading: **If you're completing this manually**

Body: Two to three sentences that describe what SBE does for this specific workflow — not a feature list, a direct answer to the exact pain just surfaced.

Closing line: "The single-venue plan is AUD $49/month. Founding member rates are locked in for life."

CTA: One plain hyperlink — "Lock in your rate → servebyexample.co/pricing"

**What the pivot does not do:**

- No pop-ups, modals, or interruptive elements
- No "upgrade now" or "try free" language
- No feature comparison tables
- No testimonials (those belong on the landing page)
- SBE is named three times maximum across the entire toolkit: once in the intro, once in a gap callout, once in the resolution block

## Regulatory scope — state-by-state RSA log

**Jurisdictions covered:** All 8 — NSW, VIC, QLD, SA, WA, TAS, ACT, NT.

**Priority tier** (highest regulatory variation, largest venue populations):

- NSW, VIC, QLD — most prescriptive incident register requirements; majority of Australian licensed venues

**Secondary tier** (covered but lighter variation):

- SA, WA — clear legislative requirements, smaller venue population
- ACT, TAS, NT — included for completeness; simpler but non-trivial requirements

**Where real variation lies:**

| Variable | Range |
|---|---|
| Mandatory fields in incident register | Prescribed by statute (NSW, QLD) vs. licensee discretion (some others) |
| Record retention period | 12 months to 3 years depending on jurisdiction |
| Who must hold RSA | All service staff (most states) vs. more limited scope (NT) |
| RSA refresher interval | NSW: 5-year renewal; other states vary or have none |
| Register availability | On-premises for inspection (universal) vs. submitted to regulator (varies) |

**Accuracy commitment:**

The template is a practical compliance guide, not a legal instrument. It must be accurate enough to function as a confident working tool — not so prescriptive that a regulatory change renders it misleading.

Every state section carries a standard caveat block: *"Requirements current as at [Month Year]. Confirm current obligations with [state regulator name + link]."*

Phase 2 requires a dedicated research pass across each state's current Liquor Act and associated regulations before any section of Asset 1 is written.
